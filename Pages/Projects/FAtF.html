Overview

  "Flip Against the Frontier is an action-packed arcade pinball game where you play as a character inside the infamously difficult pinball machine, The Frontier. You must destroy enemies, build gizmos, and survive as long as possible to reach the highest score you can!

  This project was part of the 2023 Ubisoft Game Lab Competition in Montreal. We had a max of eight developers with ten weeks of production from the release of the theme, Arcade, and the mandate which required at least one gravity mechanic, one creation mechanic, and one destruction mechanic as well as a leaderboard. Our team consisted of three programmers, two artists, two designers, and one producer."

Gameplay

  "Each programmer on the project was a gameplay programmer in many aspects but I had the heaviest burden of those features. I created our movement system since the Unity CharacterController class was too narrow for our use case. Additionally, I made the majority of the machine components like the multiball, gizmos like the bomb copter and bumpers, and the flippers. I was also responsible for the slam tilt, one of our main mechanics as it fulfilled the mandate for gravity modulation.

  Of those, one of the features I am most proud of is the slam tilt. The early problem that we had to tackle was how to keep the player, ball, or other dynamic elements from clipping when rotating the playing field. Very quickly, before any code was written, I reached the conclusion that rotating the playing field was not a viable option. Not just because of the clipping, but also because tilting could easily cause the ball and enemies to pop up off the playing field and possibly fall over the side. Ultimately I decided that it wasn't the laying field that needed to rotate, but the player and their surroundings. If I could rotate the surroundings, the player, and the camera correctly, then the player would never know the difference. There were several times I even tricked myself into thinking the machine was tilted while playing."

Diegetic UI

  "Something we knew from the beginning was that we wanted to have a lot of diegetic menus and UI. We realized that the game became unmanageable if all of the UI was diegetic in worldspace and could not come up with ways to display the rest of the information diegetically so we had to have a HUD after all, but we still managed to get the menus and the score in a place we wanted. We also wanted more settings as well, but I did not have enough time to implement them all and our producer decided that they were out of scope and we moved on to fixing bugs and polishing the game. We were very happy with the outcome.

  The UI on the backboard is simply worldspace text boxes with a font and some bloom to make it appear as though the text is made of glowing lights, but the menus were more complicated. Since we used Cinemachine for our cameras, some of the heavy-lifting was done for us. I created a system that combined Unity's EventSystem and Cinemachine so the player could switch to separate menus in other parts of the machine. Some challenges were learning Cinemachine and dealing with difficulties that arose from Unity's Input System meshing with it's UI systems."

Graphics

  "While I have had experience in graphics before, this was my first foray into being the official graphics programmer on a project. This manifested itself in many ways. I would have liked to use HLSL and write my own shaders from scratch, but we did not have the time for me to do that. I am more familiar with GLSL and had not yet learned how to use Unity's specific shader code. Instead, I opted to use shadergraph mostly because of the benefit of speed for prototyping and implementation. I created many effects and helper shaders. While it is hard to separate VFX and Animation Programming from the more generic graphics, I will do my best.

  One of my favorite shaders to work on which blurs the borders of visual effects and graphics programming is the hex-shield shader. Shields are one of my favorite effects because they can be made so unique and configurable. Every shield can look different in some way. It goes a long way to replace an invisible wall with something more tangible. Since the theme of our game was more tech-based, it wasn't a stretch to incorporate shields. Plus I really like the hexagonal patterns. I wanted this shader to have a lot of options so that the designers and artists could tweak it if they wanted to.

  I helped to give the artists more options in their custom shaders by adding simple things like color and emission multipliers, but I also created effects like the blurring around the machine. We wanted the player to focus on the gameplay and the inside of the machine. Additionally, we did not want to spend too much time on making things outside the machine have high fidelity, polished visuals since they were ultimately cosmetic and, again, not the focus of the gameplay. We originally tried using Depth of Field for a quick blur, but obviously, the intensity of the blur changed with distance so if we wanted the player to see the backboard of the machine clearly and not the person playing the game at any point, we needed to come up with a different solution. This ended up being a simple box blur applied to six planes that surround the machine. This way we can include the entire machine and the pause menu and not worry about accidental blurring."

Animation + Inverse Kinematics

  "Animations were at the forefront of our worries for a while. Early on, we knew that we would need to incorporate inverse kinematics from the feedback our mentors gave us. When there is a slanted surface that a character stands on, it is very obvious when their feet do not align with the surface they are on. We also knew that we would have to nail the animations in order to get a good feeling on the character side of the 3 C's. The issue was that animations, of course, would come much later in the project so that work had to be postponed.

  Once we had our model, we started with basic mixamo animations as placeholders so that the main portion of the programming side could be completed. Our character artist, Justin, would take the mixamo animations and touch them up to match exactly what we needed. This meant that I was free to complete all the logic for animation programming. I used Unity's state machine system to link the animations together. I used their IK system to make the character's shoes align with the surface they stood on. Eventually, we realized a slight problem. What happens to the character's legs and animations if they start walking while swinging the bat or charging the bat for a swing? To answer this, I used Unity's avatar masks and crafted the logic between the lower and upper halves of the character's body. Unfortunately, as new animations came in and changes were applied to the original model, I realized just how complicated this system could get. Still, the animations turned out *almost* perfect in the end.

  What was really exciting was the applications of what I had learned in the process of programming the character's animations. With my newfound knowledge of inverse kinematics, I realized that animating the big brother, the person playing the pinball machine, would be much easier. Originally, we planned to have a dark silhouette standing still in front of the machine since we had to explain that part somehow. I managed to convince the team of my idea and our character artist gave me a model of a man he was working on for his character art class. I used an idle animation and had the hands attach to the side of the machine. During the animation, he would move side to side occasionally and it looked very organic. I had the head look at the first unlocked ball and, if no unlocked balls were on the field, then he would look at the player. Lastly, during the slam tilt, I had him rotate only about 75% of the amount that the room tilted around the machine. This gave the illusion that he was moving the machine. The blurring used around the machine also allowed Justin to do a rush job on the texture so that this cosmetic feature did not take too much development time. In total, this feature took me less than an hour and a half. It was another one of those smaller features we didn't originally plan, but once it was in the game, we couldn't imagine not having it."

Sound Implementation

  "The sound on Flip Against the Frontier was mainly outsourced. One of our designers kept in contact with sound designers on Champlain's main campus and made sure all the sounds got made, but programmer assistance was needed to actually play the sounds in-game. Since there was not much time, we opted for a singleton pattern so that accessing sounds was easy and so that designers only had to look in one place for all the sounds. I knew that we would be sacrificing flexibility that would normally end up costing more time later, but I was confident that the scope of our project did not require that flexibility and that development time would suffer from implementing it. In fact, the pattern worked to our benefit later when we implemented a volume system. It did not take long since all the sounds went through the singleton. The only things I would change if I could do it again would be to use more events throughout the code-base so that designers could implement new sounds more easily rather than having to play most sounds through code and to make a simpler process for adding new sounds. Of course, if I had more time, I may have tried a better system than a singleton as well.

  I created a tool to give designers as much control over each sound as possible. The sound system functioned on scriptable objects called SoundEffects. Each sound effect is a separate sound that is triggered by some event. It supported multiple clips that would be randomly selected each time the SoundEffect was played. This was mainly for things like footsteps that would be played repetitively and gave some uniqueness to each sound. The pitch could be randomized within the range specified by the pitch modulation range and there were options for things like base volume, sound type (for volume settings), whether or not the sound is looped, and a falloff distance category that had some presets for common falloff ranges (like enemies and machine parts) but supported custom falloff curves that used animation curves."

VFX Creation + Implementation

  "VFX is a newfound passion of mine that I did not realize I had. As the project was coming into the last few weeks and art was being implemented, we realized there was still a lot of texturing and modeling that needed to be done. We were not sure whether or not the artists would have time to do all the VFX so we decided to pivot. Since I was finishing my tasks fairly quickly, VFX became my stretch goals and I started making them whenever I had time. I ended up making about half the VFX in the final build. It ended up being the right choice because, even once artists started working on VFX, we were still creating them up until last week of the project. I became very familiar with Unity's particle system, Visual Effect Graph, and Shadergraph from working on VFX. Once artists were able to work on the VFX, I had to turn my focus to implementing all of those VFX and fixing bugs.

  Some of my favorite effects I made were the spawning of enemies, impact effects when hitting the pinball, the attracting lines between the bat and the ball, the magnet tower's attracting effect, and the hologram buttons in the UI. Each time I started a new effect, the creative freedom made me realize why artists seem to love their jobs so much. We made sure that developing each of the visual effects had an impact on the gameplay somehow. A big effect was hitting the pinball. For the charged swing (at the request of designers) I combined a lot of visual and haptic effects like camera shake, controller rumble, impact lines on the edge of the screen, and freeze frame to make hitting the ball as satisfying and impactful as possible. The only part of that effect I did not do was the sparks emanating from the bat which was done by our environment artist, Maddi."

Reflection

  "This project is the largest and longest I have ever worked on in the realm of game development. It was a source of many sleepless nights for many of us. In the span of 10 weeks, I contributed over 350 hours to the project on top of other classes and projects. Often, class projects are 1-4 weeks and game jams I've participated in have short time frames as well. It was hard to know how to approach this project because we had to decide what to do first and how to scope for almost triple the amount of time we normally had. Additionally, we had to fit all of this within the constraints of the theme and mandate of the Ubisoft Game Lab Competition. With a theme of arcade and seemingly contradictory mandate of creation and destruction mechanics, the concepting phase was very difficult.

  We originally had two concepts. One was a 2.5D beat-em-up game and in the other, you played as a janitor in a pinball machine cleaning up messes and destroying old bumpers and parts of the machine with new ones. We actually had a split vote in our team because neither concept was well thought out at the time. Our product owner made the final decision to go with the pinball game. While some on the team were not very happy with the decision, we all remained professional and worked with the concept we chose. We went through a lot of iteration because the original concept was fairly weak. With the help of our mentors from Ubisoft, we managed to develop enemies for second-to-second gameplay whereas the pinball is seen less often.

  We contextualized the gameplay as Flip, who is part of a family of tech geniuses, shrinking herself into the pinball machine to help her big brother get the high score on the infamously difficult pinball game, The Frontier. The enemies, known as drain dwellers, are the machine's anti-cheat system coming after Flip for cheating. We also added a magnet-like ability where the player could draw in the ball by holding down right trigger to charge their swing. Even the tilting of the machine received an overhaul halfway through the project. Instead of simply tilting, it is now more like the machine is slammed to one side or the other and enemies are thrown into the wall and destroyed. This allows it to be used as a board-clearing tool to help players in a pinch.

  Something that surprised me was all of the smaller features that brought the game together. It is amazing how there are so many things we almost didn't add that completely changed the atmosphere of the pinball machine, the menus, or any of our many systems. It was a combination of many features that often go unnoticed when they are there, like IK for the feet and a person playing the pinball machine, but you would notice very quickly if it was missing. In the future, I will be sure to place more emphasis on the polish phase since it adds so much to the game.

  If I were to do anything differently, I would have increased my communication with designers earlier in the project. While I struggled to do so early in the project to follow documentation, I quickly learned to closely follow it to see how a mechanic is supposed to work. However, often I would create a mechanic to match those specifications and later be told that the documentation is out of date. A lot of development time could have been saved if I had directly asked designers about the features I was working on. By the end of the project, our communication was much stronger and we worked as a cohesive unit. Something I think we could have improved on as a team was our concepting speed. In a ten-week project, it is important to iron out the concept early, but we had a disorganized process and lost the first week of concepting. In the second week, we brainstormed and allowed the designers to create concepts from our ideas. Unfortunately, these too were unpolished and we spent a lot of development time finding the fun in our game. Often it seems the concepting phase is discounted, but we have learned that it is one of the most critical junctures in a games development and will not underestimate it in the future.
